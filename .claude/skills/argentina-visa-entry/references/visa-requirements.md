# Argentina Visa Requirements by Nationality

> **Important**: Entry requirements change. Verify with the Argentine consulate or www.migraciones.gov.ar before travel. This document reflects general patterns as of mid-2024 — always confirm current status.

---

## Visa-Free Entry (90 Days Tourism)

The following nationalities can enter Argentina as tourists without a prior visa for up to 90 days:

### European Union & Schengen
All EU/EEA citizens: Austria, Belgium, Bulgaria, Croatia, Cyprus, Czech Republic, Denmark, Estonia, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Hungary, Iceland, Ireland, Italy, Latvia, Liechtenstein, Lithuania, Luxembourg, Malta, Netherlands, Norway, Poland, Portugal, Romania, Slovakia, Slovenia, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland.
- Standard entry: passport + onward ticket (proof may be requested)

### English-Speaking / Common Travel Countries
- **United Kingdom**: Visa-free, 90 days
- **United States**: Visa-free, 90 days (reciprocity fee historically applied but was lifted — verify current status)
- **Canada**: Visa-free, 90 days (verify reciprocity fee status — fluctuates with policy)
- **Australia**: Visa-free, 90 days (reciprocity fee historically applied — verify current status)
- **New Zealand**: Visa-free, 90 days

### South American Nationals (Mercosur & Associates)
Citizens of Mercosur member and associate states can enter with national ID (cedula) — passport may not be required:
- **Brazil, Uruguay, Paraguay, Chile, Bolivia, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, Venezuela**: Entry with national document; 90 days; easy extension possible
- **Mexico**: Visa-free, 90 days

### Other Visa-Free Nationalities (Non-Exhaustive)
- Japan, South Korea, Israel, South Africa (90 days)
- Most Latin American countries

## Visa Required Prior to Arrival

The following nationalities generally require a visa obtained at an Argentine consulate before travel:
- China (PRC), India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Nigeria, Egypt, Iran, most Middle Eastern passports
- Check with the nearest Argentine consulate — some nationalities have specific bilateral agreements

## Reciprocity Fee (Tasas de Reciprocidad)

Argentina has historically imposed entry fees on nationals of countries that charged Argentines for visas — most notably US, Canadian, and Australian citizens.
- This fee was **suspended for US citizens** (2016) and later for others
- **Always verify current status** as the fee can be reinstated by presidential decree
- When active, the fee was paid online before arrival at a specific government portal
- USD 160 (USA), USD 75 (Canada), USD 100 (Australia) were historical rates — not currently active as of 2024 but subject to reinstatement

## Stay Duration & Extensions

### Standard Tourist Stay
- **90 days** for visa-free nationals from most Western countries
- Mercosur nationals typically get 90 days or more with easier renewal

### Extending Your Stay
Option 1 — **Exit and Re-entry**: Leave Argentina (e.g., cross to Colonia Uruguay by ferry, or to Chile) and re-enter. Most travellers do this once for another 90 days. Technically, immigration officers have discretion to question pattern of "visa runs" — not usually an issue for genuine tourists.

Option 2 — **Formal Extension (Prórroga)**: Apply at Dirección Nacional de Migraciones in Buenos Aires (offices in Retiro area) for a 90-day extension. Cost is a small administrative fee. Requires:
- Valid passport
- Return/onward ticket or explanation of travel plans
- Proof of funds (bank statement helpful)
- No criminal record

Option 3 — **Digital Nomad Visa**: Argentina introduced a specific visa for remote workers. Requirements include proof of foreign-source income above a minimum threshold (check current amount) and valid health insurance. Allows longer stays and potential tax simplification.

## Passport Validity Requirements

- Argentina does not publish a formal "must be valid for 6 months beyond your stay" rule but immigration officers may deny entry if your passport expires within 6 months of entry — follow the 6-month rule as best practice.
- South American nationals entering with national ID cards: ID must be valid at time of entry.
- Damaged or heavily worn passports: May be refused at discretion of officer — travel with a well-maintained document.

---

## Entry for Minors (Under 18)

- **Travelling with both parents**: Standard entry, no special documentation required.
- **Travelling with one parent**: Argentine immigration can be strict about children's travel. The absent parent should provide:
  - A notarised authorisation letter (autorizacion de viaje) stating permission to travel
  - Ideally apostilled if international
  - Certified translation into Spanish
- **Travelling alone or with a non-parent guardian**: Full notarised authorisation from both parents required + guardian's documents proving relationship or legal guardianship
- This applies to Argentine citizens, foreign citizens transiting through Argentina, and arriving in Argentina

## Health Requirements

### Yellow Fever Certificate
Required for travellers arriving **from or via** yellow fever endemic countries:
- If arriving from Brazil, Bolivia, Peru, Colombia, Venezuela, Paraguay, Ecuador, or other endemic African/South American countries: Present ICVP (International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis)
- The certificate must have been issued **at least 10 days before arrival**
- If no certificate: May be required to receive vaccination on arrival or refused entry

If your itinerary includes visiting Brazil (Iguazu Brazilian side) before Argentina, vaccination is recommended regardless.

### Other Health Documents
- No other routine health certificates required for most nationalities
- Check latest requirements close to travel date for any temporary measures (post-pandemic protocols, etc.)
- Travel health insurance: Not mandated by Argentina but recommended; Argentine private hospitals are good quality but charge foreigners at full rates

## Land Border Crossings

### From Chile
Major crossings (all require valid travel documents — no national ID for non-Mercosur):
- **Los Libertadores** (near Mendoza ↔ Santiago): Busiest; main highway; occasionally closed in heavy snow (Jun–Sep)
- **Paso Samoré** (Bariloche ↔ Osorno): Usually open; some seasonal closures
- **Paso Integración Austral** (Río Grande ↔ Punta Arenas): Tierra del Fuego crossing
- Cruce de Lagos boat crossing (Bariloche ↔ Puerto Montt): Scenic tourist crossing; passport stamped at border points along the route

### From Uruguay
- **Gualeguaychú – Fray Bentos bridge**: Road crossing
- **Buenos Aires – Colonia del Sacramento**: Buquebus ferry (3 hrs to Colonia; 2.5 hrs to Montevideo); most common tourist route
  - Buy Buquebus tickets in advance; popular, sells out weekends

### From Brazil
- **Puerto Iguazu – Foz do Iguaçu**: Daily crossing; must check Brazil visa requirement for your nationality; not all visa-free Argentina nationalities are visa-free for Brazil

### From Bolivia
- **La Quiaca – Villazón**: Main northern crossing; used by budget travellers on overland South America route

### From Paraguay
- **Posadas – Encarnación**: Via bridge; Jesuits missions circuit
