# Argentina Culture, Customs & Safety

## Social Customs & Etiquette

### Greetings
- **Greeting style**: One kiss on the right cheek — universal between women, and men-to-women. Between men, a handshake or sometimes also a cheek kiss depending on familiarity.
- This applies between strangers being introduced — do not be surprised; reciprocate naturally.
- In business settings: handshake is standard; kiss is for social situations.

### The Mate Ritual
- Mate (yerba mate tea) is central to Argentine social culture. It is shared from a single gourd passed around a group, drunk through a metal straw (bombilla).
- **Etiquette**: If offered mate, you drink the full gourd then pass it back to the cebador (server), who refills it. Do not say "thank you" (gracias) until you want no more — saying gracias means you are finished.
- Refusing mate when offered by locals is considered mildly rude. Politely participating is a gesture of respect and goodwill.
- Mate is drunk plain (amargo), sweet (dulce), or with herbs. It's very bitter — don't grimace.

### Dining Culture
- **Meal times**: Lunch is 1–3pm; dinner is 9pm–midnight. Eating at 6–7pm marks you as a tourist — restaurants may be literally empty.
- **Asado (barbecue)**: The central social institution of Argentina. Invitations to an asado at someone's home are a sign of genuine hospitality — accept if possible.
- **Restaurant behaviour**: Do not rush — long leisurely meals are the norm. Asking for the bill (la cuenta) is not rude but waiters will not bring it unsolicited.
- **Bread and appetisers**: Usually charged (cubierto cover charge) — don't be surprised by a small line item on the bill.

### Personal Space & Conversation
- Argentines (especially portenos) are expressive, engaged conversationalists. Interrupting lightly is normal and not considered rude.
- Topics: Football (use caution — club loyalty is fierce; don't joke about River vs Boca), the economy (universal topic, discussed openly), family, food. Avoid Falklands/Malvinas politically.
- Formality: Argentines use "vos" (not "tú") for second-person singular. First names are used quickly. Usted (formal you) is rare except with elderly or in official contexts.

### Tipping
- **Restaurants**: 10–15% tip is standard for good service (propina). Not automatically added — leave cash on the table.
- **Taxis / Uber**: Rounding up is customary; not expected to tip heavily.
- **Hotel porters**: $2–5 USD equivalent in pesos per bag.
- **Tour guides**: $5–10 USD equivalent per person for half-day; $10–20 for full day.
- Tips almost always in cash (pesos) — card tip boxes are rare.

### Time & Punctuality
- Social plans run 30–60 minutes late by convention (hora argentina). Showing up exactly on time to a dinner party is early.
- Business appointments run approximately on time (within 15 min); tourist activities and tours run on schedule.

## Safety by Region

### Buenos Aires — Overall: Moderate
Buenos Aires is a large city with normal urban risks. The vast majority of visitors have trouble-free stays.

**Safe for tourists (standard precautions apply)**:
- Palermo, Recoleta, Belgrano, Puerto Madero, San Telmo (daytime + evening)
- Most restaurant and hotel areas

**Exercise extra caution**:
- La Boca: Stay strictly on the Caminito tourist corridor. The surrounding blocks change character quickly — do not wander off the painted pedestrian path, especially after dark.
- Once, Constitución, parts of the Microcentro: Higher petty theft risk, especially at night.
- Street protests (piquetes): Common and generally peaceful, but can disrupt transport. Avoid large crowds if tear gas is deployed.

**Common Buenos Aires scams**:
- **Mustard scam**: A stranger "accidentally" squirts mustard/ketchup/bird dropping on you, a helper appears to clean it — and pickpockets your valuables. Do not stop; walk away briskly.
- **Fake police**: Someone approaches showing a badge claiming to check for counterfeit dollars. Real police do not do this. Walk away; counterfeit dollar checks happen at banks.
- **Shortchange at markets**: Count your change at street stalls. Count peso notes carefully — bundles of 100s are sometimes missing notes.
- **Express kidnapping (secuestro express)**: Rare but exists; take authorised taxis (remises), use InDriver/Cabify app, avoid flagging taxis at night.

**Practical precautions**:
- Keep phone in a front pocket or bag with a zip, not visibly in hand while walking
- Use money belt for passports and large cash; keep daily spending cash in a separate pocket
- Download InDriver or Cabify (ride-share apps) — safer than street taxis at night
- SUBE card for metro and bus — safer than carrying cash on public transport
- ATMs: Use machines inside banks or shopping centres, not street-facing ATMs at night

### Mendoza — Overall: Low Risk
- Generally calm and tourist-friendly
- Wine region areas very safe; exercise normal caution in city centre at night
- Car theft exists — don't leave valuables in rental cars

### Patagonia (Bariloche, El Calafate, Ushuaia) — Overall: Low Risk
- Very safe for tourists; low crime
- Main risks are environmental: weather, altitude, getting lost on trails
- Register at national park visitors centres before trekking (mandatory in El Chaltén)
- Tell accommodation when and where you plan to hike

### Salta — Overall: Low–Moderate
- City centre is safe; tourist zone well-patrolled
- Exercise standard caution at bus terminal area and outer suburbs
- Night bus connections: reputable companies (Andesmar, Flecha Bus) are safe

### Iguazu — Overall: Low Risk (Puerto Iguazu town)
- Town is small and tourist-oriented; generally safe
- Brazilian side: Puerto Iguazu → Foz do Iguaçu crossing is safe for park visit with tour; the city of Foz do Iguaçu has higher crime — stay on organised tours

## Money & Payments

### Currency Situation
- Argentina has experienced significant currency instability. The "official rate" vs "blue rate" (parallel market rate) has historically diverged significantly.
- Check the current exchange situation before travel — regulations change frequently.
- **Carry USD cash** in small denominations ($1, $5, $10, $20) — often used for informal exchange at authorised exchange houses (casas de cambio).
- **Card payments**: Increasingly common in cities; domestic debit cards charged at official rate (historically less favourable). Confirm with your bank before travel.

### Cash Availability
- ATMs are common in cities but often have low withdrawal limits per transaction and may charge fees
- **El Chaltén has no ATM** — withdraw sufficient cash in El Calafate
- Remote areas of Patagonia and Northwest: Carry extra cash buffers

### Bargaining
- Not part of mainstream Argentine culture in restaurants or shops
- Minor negotiation acceptable at artisan markets (ferias artesanales)
- Taxi/remise prices are metered or agreed upfront — no bargaining once in vehicle
