# Argentina Food, Drink & Language

## Food Culture

### The Asado (Argentine BBQ)
- The defining culinary institution of Argentina. An asado is both the meal and the social event.
- **At a parrilla restaurant**: Meats cooked over hardwood or charcoal embers (not gas). The parrillero (grill master) controls the process over hours.
- **Key cuts**:
  - *Bife de chorizo*: Thick sirloin — the iconic Argentine steak; most commonly ordered
  - *Ojo de bife*: Ribeye — rich, marbled, excellent
  - *Tira de asado*: Short ribs (thin strips) — classic asado cut; fall-off-bone
  - *Vacío*: Flank steak — popular, flavourful, slightly tougher
  - *Entraña*: Skirt steak — thin, intense flavour, cook fast
  - *Mollejas*: Sweetbreads (thymus gland) — grilled crispy; a delicacy; worth trying
  - *Morcilla*: Blood sausage — served as starter at asado
  - *Chorizo*: Argentine pork/beef sausage (not spicy — very different from Spanish chorizo)
- **How to order steak**: *a punto* (medium), *jugoso* (medium rare), *bien cocido* (well done). Argentine default is medium-well — specify *jugoso* for pink.
- **Accompaniments**: Chimichurri (herb/oil/garlic sauce — always on table), salsa criolla (tomato/onion), salad.

### Empanadas
- Stuffed pastry — the ubiquitous snack and lunch food of Argentina.
- Each region has a distinct style:
  - *Buenos Aires*: baked, mild, often beef (carne) with egg and olives
  - *Salta / Jujuy*: smaller, baked, spicy beef (*saltenas*) — often considered the best
  - *Tucumán*: similar to Salta style; claims to be the empanada capital
  - *Mendoza*: sometimes with goat or lamb
  - Common fillings: carne (beef), pollo (chicken), jamón y queso (ham & cheese), humita (sweetcorn), espinaca (spinach & cheese), caprese

### Breakfast
- Traditional Argentine: medialunas (butter croissants, slightly sweet), café con leche, toast with butter and dulce de leche (caramel spread).
- Café culture: Argentine café visits are long — you can sit for hours with one coffee; no pressure to leave.
- Tostadas: Argentine toast with butter and jam, or dulce de leche (try it — excellent).

### Regional Specialities

**Buenos Aires**:
- Milanesa (breaded schnitzel — very common; milanesa napolitana with tomato + cheese is outstanding)
- Fugazza / fugazzeta (thick-crust pizza with onions — Buenos Aires pizza is thick and generous)
- Medialunas at Café Tortoni, La Biela, El Federal (classic cafés)

**Salta & Northwest**:
- *Locro*: Thick stew of white corn, beans, meat and squash — winter comfort food (May–Aug)
- *Humita en chala*: Sweetcorn paste wrapped in corn husks; earthy, sweet
- *Tamal*: Cornmeal stuffed with beef, wrapped in corn husk; similar to tamale
- *Torrontés*: White wine grape unique to the region — floral, fruity — try in Cafayate

**Patagonia**:
- *Cordero patagónico*: Whole Patagonian lamb slow-roasted on a cross (asado al palo) — outstanding; a regional speciality not to miss in El Calafate or Bariloche
- *Trucha*: Rainbow trout from Andean lakes — grilled fresh in Bariloche / San Martín de los Andes
- *Centolla*: King crab from Tierra del Fuego — expensive but exceptional in Ushuaia
- *Chocolates and craft beer*: Bariloche is famous for artisan chocolate and Patagonian craft beer

**Mendoza**:
- *Chivo a la llama*: Goat roasted over an open flame — the regional meat
- Malbec wine: World-class red; also Bonarda, Cabernet Franc
- *Humita* and *tamales* also appear in Cuyo region

**Iguazu / Northeast**:
- *Chipá*: Warm, chewy cheese bread made from tapioca flour — bought at street stalls and on buses
- *Sopa paraguaya*: Dense cornbread with cheese and onion
- Fresh tropical fruit: mango, papaya, guava readily available

### Drinks
- **Mate**: National drink — see culture-safety.md for etiquette
- **Fernet con Coca**: Mixing Fernet-Branca amaro with Coca-Cola — Argentina's unofficial national cocktail; oddly addictive; order at any bar
- **Malbec**: Default red wine of Argentina; Mendoza produces world-class examples ($3–8 for a good bottle at a supermarket)
- **Torrontés**: Unique Argentine white wine — only from Salta province; floral and fruity
- **Cerveza artesanal (craft beer)**: Booming scene; excellent in Bariloche (Berlina, Antares), Buenos Aires (Growler, 1516)
- **Submarino**: Hot milk with a chocolate bar dropped in — melt and stir; a classic café drink in winter

### Restaurant Practical Tips
- **Opening hours**: Restaurants open for lunch around 12:30–1pm, close 3–4pm. Reopen for dinner 8–8:30pm; Buenos Aires often 9pm+.
- **Cubierto (cover charge)**: Small charge per person for bread, condiments — normal; shown on menu
- **Water**: Ask for "agua con gas" (sparkling) or "agua sin gas" (still) — both are charged. Tap water in Buenos Aires and Mendoza is safe to drink.
- **Wine by glass**: *copa de vino* — often excellent value at parrillas; half-bottle (*media botella*) common
- **Bill**: Ask "*la cuenta, por favor*" — they will not bring it automatically

### Where to Eat — Finding Local vs Tourist Restaurants
**Good signs of a local restaurant**: Full at 9pm; menu in Spanish only or minimal English; no photos on menu; specials written on a chalkboard; mains under $15 USD equivalent.
**Red flags for tourist trap**: Menu in 4 languages on laminated boards outside; touts at the door; photos of every dish; "authentic" in English on the sign.

## Language: Argentine Spanish

### The Vos System
- Argentine Spanish uses *vos* instead of *tú* for "you" (second-person singular). Verb conjugations change slightly.
  - "You speak" = *vos hablás* (not *tú hablas*)
  - "You are" = *vos sos* (not *tú eres*)
- Locals will understand *tú* but using *vos* shows cultural awareness.

### Argentine Slang (Lunfardo)

| Slang | Meaning | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| *Che* | Hey / mate (address) | "Che, ¿cómo estás?" (Hey, how are you?) |
| *Boludo/a* | Buddy (among friends); insult (to strangers) | Context-sensitive — use only with close friends |
| *Pibe/a* | Kid / guy / girl | "El pibe ese" (that guy) |
| *Copado/a* | Cool, great | "¡Qué copado!" (How cool!) |
| *Quilombo* | Mess, chaos, trouble | "Hay un quilombo en la calle" (there's chaos in the street) |
| *Laburo* | Work / job | "Tengo mucho laburo" (I have a lot of work) |
| *Mango* | Peso (informal for money) | "¿Tenés un mango?" (Got any cash?) |
| *Fiaca* | Laziness, can't-be-bothered feeling | "Tengo fiaca" (I can't be bothered) |
| *Buena onda* | Good vibes / cool person | "Tiene buena onda" (She has good vibes) |
| *Dale* | OK / sure / let's go | Very multipurpose; equivalent of "yeah" |
| *¿Cómo andás?* | How are you going? | Standard greeting (more common than *¿cómo estás?*) |

### Pronunciation Tips
- **ll / y** are pronounced like the English "sh" in Buenos Aires (distinct from other Spanish): "llave" = "sha-veh", "yo" = "sho"
- **s** is always aspirated (never dropped as in Caribbean Spanish)
- Stress tends to fall on the last syllable for vos verb forms: *hablás*, *comés*, *vivís*
